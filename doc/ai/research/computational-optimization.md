# Computational Approaches to Prospective Training Plan Optimization

Computational Approaches to Prospective Training Plan Optimization: Integrating the Banister Impulse-Response Model into GoldenCheetah

## 1. Introduction and the Paradigm Shift to Forward SimulationThe optimization of endurance sports training is fundamentally an exercise in managing a complex, non-linear dose-response relationship. The central objective is to administer physiological stimuli of sufficient magnitude and frequency to provoke positive structural and functional adaptations, while simultaneously permitting adequate recovery to dissipate the neuromuscular and metabolic fatigue induced by the stimuli. In the domain of computational sports science, the quantification and modeling of this dynamic relationship have been heavily influenced by the impulse-response model originally proposed by Banister et al. in 1975, widely known as the Fitness-Fatigue Model (FFM).Within the current architecture of GoldenCheetah, an advanced open-source performance analysis platform, the Banister FFM is implemented strictly as a retrospective analytical instrument. The software calculates an individual athlete's distinct performance parameters by utilizing Levenberg-Marquardt least-squares regression to fit the model against historical training loads and periodic maximal performance tests (e.g., Critical Power field tests or peak power index values). While this retrospective fitting yields valuable visualizations of an athlete's historical fitness and fatigue trajectories, the resulting mathematical parameters remain entirely isolated from GoldenCheetah's workout generation ecosystem. Presently, the workout generation system relies on static, population-generalized heuristics. It applies a uniform Chronic Training Load (CTL) scaling factor to adjust interval counts, utilizing a bounded multiplier (ctlFactor = clamp(0.6, CTL / 70.0, 1.5)), and assigns fixed power zones irrespective of an individual’s unique physiological decay rates or specific adaptation magnitudes.Transitioning the Banister model from a backward-looking visualization feature to a forward-simulating training optimizer represents a high-leverage architectural paradigm shift. By conceptualizing the athlete's current physiological state as the initial conditions within a deterministic dynamical system, the fitted model parameters can be leveraged to project performance trajectories into the future. This enables the algorithmic simulation of thousands of candidate training macrocycles, facilitating the selection of a prescriptive plan that mathematically maximizes a target performance metric on a specific race day, subject to physiological safety constraints. This report provides an exhaustive, multi-disciplinary analysis of the mathematical mechanics, algorithmic optimization frameworks, physiological limitations, and software architecture required to seamlessly integrate forward training simulation into GoldenCheetah.

## 2. Mathematical Dynamics and the Limitations of the Linear Banister ModelTo accurately deploy the Banister impulse-response model as a prospective engine, its mathematical foundations and inherent boundary conditions must be rigorously delineated. The foundational premise of the FFM is that any applied training dose acts as a systemic impulse that simultaneously initiates a slow-decaying positive physiological adaptation and a fast-decaying negative systemic fatigue.The classical governing equation expresses the predicted performance capability $p(t)$ at any discrete temporal point $t$ as the balance between these two antagonistic transfer functions:$$p(t) = p_0 + k_1 \cdot PTE(t) - k_2 \cdot NTE(t)$$Where $p_0$ represents the athlete's baseline, pre-training performance level. The Positive Training Effect ($PTE$) and Negative Training Effect ($NTE$) are convolutions of the daily scalar training dose $w(i)$, decaying exponentially over time based on specific time constants:$$PTE(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} w(i) \cdot e^{-(t-i)/\tau_1}$$$$NTE(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} w(i) \cdot e^{-(t-i)/\tau_2}$$In this framework, $k_1$ serves as the fitness magnitude multiplier (quantifying the yield of performance gain per unit of training load), and $k_2$ acts as the fatigue magnitude multiplier (quantifying the performance decrement per unit of training load). The time constants $\tau_1$ and $\tau_2$ dictate the exponential half-life of these effects. A defining characteristic of the model is that fatigue must decay significantly faster than fitness ($\tau_1 > \tau_2$), a mathematical representation of the biological reality that acute systemic exhaustion resolves in a matter of days, whereas structural adaptations—such as mitochondrial biogenesis and capillary angiogenesis—persist for weeks or months.

### Parameter Multicollinearity and Ill-ConditioningThe transition to forward simulation is heavily constrained by the statistical stability of the underlying parameter estimation. The Banister model is notoriously susceptible to ill-conditioning during the retrospective fitting phase due to severe multicollinearity between its parameters. Extensive statistical analyses of elite swimmers and endurance athletes reveal extraordinarily high correlation coefficients between the estimated parameters. The fitness and fatigue time constants ($\tau_1$ and $\tau_2$) frequently exhibit a correlation approaching $r = 0.99 \pm 0.01$, while the magnitude multipliers ($k_1$ and $k_2$) demonstrate correlations of $r = 0.91 \pm 0.13$.This extreme collinearity implies that the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm—utilized by GoldenCheetah via the lmcurve() function—faces an ill-posed inverse problem. Multiple, mathematically divergent sets of parameters can yield near-identical fit errors across the historical training data. In a retrospective context, this ambiguity is benign; the modeled performance curve will trace the historical test data accurately regardless of the specific internal balance between the parameters. However, in a prospective simulation extending weeks into the future, parameter uncertainty creates compounding divergence. If the 95% confidence interval for $\tau_1$ spans from 17 to 59 days—as observed in certain elite swimmer cohorts—a forward simulation projecting performance 12 weeks out will yield drastically different optimal training tapers depending on the precise point estimate selected.

### The "More is Better" Mathematical ParadoxBeyond parameter instability, the core mathematical structure of the classical Banister model presents a severe logical flaw when subjected to optimization algorithms. The model is inherently linear concerning the training dose; the fitness yield ($k_1$) and the fatigue cost ($k_2$) remain perfectly constant regardless of the accumulated load.If an unconstrained mathematical optimizer is deployed to find the training schedule that maximizes $p(t_{race})$, the linear Banister model dictates a physiological impossibility: it concludes that the optimal strategy is to train at the maximum allowable limit every single day until a brief tapering period begins. Because the first derivative of the fitness function with respect to training load never turns negative, the model lacks an intrinsic mechanism for threshold saturation or diminishing returns. In biological reality, excessive, unrelenting training load inevitably triggers a cascade of maladaptations, immunosuppression, and overtraining syndrome. Thus, deploying the linear Banister equation as a forward simulator requires the superimposition of rigid, extrinsic physiological constraints to prevent the generation of destructive training prescriptions.

## 3. Forward Simulation Feasibility and Predictive HorizonEvaluating the literature surrounding the prospective, rather than retrospective, use of impulse-response models is critical to validating the proposed architecture for GoldenCheetah. While the challenges of multicollinearity are well-documented, empirical evidence demonstrates that carefully calibrated FFM simulations can achieve statistically significant predictive accuracy within bounded temporal horizons.

### Empirical Accuracy and Temporal BoundariesStudies assessing the forward-predictive capability of the Banister model indicate that it functions effectively as a medium-term oracle for performance trends. When calibrated against 15 to 20 weeks of highly structured historical training data, the model has been shown to accurately predict athlete performance across subsequent 8-week mesocycles.In robust cross-validation experiments utilizing elite athletes, the standard Banister model achieved a Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) of $2.02 \pm 0.65\%$ for short-term (8-week) prediction horizons, and a MAPE of $2.69 \pm 1.23\%$ for long-term (season-wide) predictions. Given that the inherent measurement error of commercial bicycle strain-gauge power meters frequently ranges between 1.0% and 2.0%, a predictive MAPE of approximately 2% suggests that the model's accuracy is closely aligned with the limits of hardware measurement fidelity.However, despite these tight error margins, researchers routinely caution against using the absolute wattage output of a forward simulation as an exact quantitative target. Because day-to-day variances in physiological readiness, environmental conditions, and psychological state are unmodeled variables, the precise numerical prediction must be viewed probabilistically. The true utility of the forward simulation lies in comparative optimization: it excels at determining the directional superiority of one training plan over another. Even if the absolute projected Critical Power of 320W carries a $\pm 8W$ margin of error, the forward simulator can reliably deduce whether a high-volume polarized strategy will yield a higher performance peak than a threshold-heavy block.

### Calibration Requirements and Signal-to-Noise RatiosTo achieve a viable forward prediction horizon, the density and temporal distribution of the historical performance tests are paramount. GoldenCheetah currently mandates a minimum of two performance test data points per fitting window to generate parameter estimates. The literature strongly suggests this is insufficient for stable prospective optimization. A highly sparse performance dataset allows the Levenberg-Marquardt optimizer to overfit to localized noise. To isolate the true physiological transfer functions from environmental noise, the model requires regular, sub-maximal and maximal probing across diverse energy systems, ideally incorporating continuous performance inferences generated from stochastic real-world racing and unstructured high-intensity group rides.

## 4. Simulation-Based Plan Optimization AlgorithmsTo operationalize forward simulation within GoldenCheetah, the core mathematical challenge involves searching a vast, multi-dimensional combinatorial space. The objective is to identify the precise sequence of daily training loads that maximizes the target function $p(t_{race})$, subject to a complex array of non-linear physiological constraints. Three primary optimization algorithms dominate the sports science literature for this task: Particle Swarm Optimization, Genetic Algorithms, and Dynamic Programming.

### 4.1. Adaptive Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)Particle Swarm Optimization is a stochastic, population-based metaheuristic inspired by the emergent behavioral dynamics of flocking birds. In the context of training plan generation, a single "particle" represents a complete, multi-week training plan. For a standard 8-week macrocycle, each particle is encoded as a 112-dimensional continuous array, with each dimension pair representing the intensity (e.g., target heart rate or normalized power) and duration of a single daily session.The algorithm initializes a diverse swarm of randomized training plans. In each computational iteration, every particle evaluates its fitness by running the Banister forward simulation to determine its resulting performance score at $t_{race}$. The particle then updates its multi-dimensional velocity vector—adjusting its sequence of daily durations and intensities—based on a combination of its own historical best-performing plan (cognitive component) and the single best plan discovered by the entire swarm (social component).To ensure the algorithm converges on realistic plans rather than chaotic oscillations, Adaptive PSO dynamically scales the inertia weight of the particles. During early iterations, particles traverse the solution space with high velocity, broadly exploring distinct periodization strategies. In later iterations, the velocity is severely constrained, shifting the algorithm from global exploration to localized fine-tuning of the optimal taper protocol.

### 4.2. Genetic Algorithms (GA) and Evolutionary ComputingGenetic Algorithms operate by simulating Darwinian evolutionary mechanics. An initial population of heuristic-based candidate training plans is generated, and their prospective performance outcomes are evaluated via the FFM objective function. The algorithm then executes tournament selection, isolating the highest-performing training plans to serve as "parents" for the next generation.Through crossover operations, the algorithm might splice the high-volume base phase of Parent A with the high-intensity peaking phase of Parent B to create a novel offspring plan. Mutation operators are subsequently applied, introducing slight randomized perturbations—such as converting a recovery day into an endurance day or extending an interval duration by five minutes—to ensure continuous genetic diversity and prevent the algorithm from collapsing into local physiological optima. Open-source evolutionary frameworks, such as the Python-based PonyGE2 library, have been successfully deployed to evolve in-season training models for elite field-sport athletes and competitive swimmers, demonstrating robust resistance to the ill-conditioning traps that plague gradient-based optimizers.

### 4.3. Dynamic Programming and Optimal ControlWhen training prescription is framed strictly as an optimal control problem, Dynamic Programming (DP) techniques, such as the Forward-Backward Sweep method, become highly effective. In this framework, the objective function seeks to maximize fitness while actively minimizing accumulated fatigue precisely on the competition day, simultaneously seeking to minimize the aggregate cumulative training load required to reach that state.DP algorithms discretize the temporal horizon into distinct state grids. Utilizing the Bellman equation, the algorithm works recursively backward from the target race day, calculating the optimal marginal load for each preceding day. This approach is uniquely suited for navigating highly constrained, multi-objective environments where the cost function heavily penalizes unnecessary physiological strain.

## 5. Alternative Non-Linear and State-Event ModelsGiven the fundamental requirement to manually code numerous boundary constraints to force the linear Banister model into biological compliance, the GoldenCheetah architecture should be designed with sufficient modularity to support alternative physiological models. Advanced non-linear models intrinsically account for the physiological realities of threshold saturation and overtraining, reducing the reliance on external algorithmic guardrails.

### 5.1. The Busso (2003) Variable Dose-Response ModelTo correct the "more is better" fallacy inherent in the linear FFM, Busso proposed a highly influential non-linear modification. In the Busso (2003) variable dose-response model, the fatigue magnitude multiplier ($k_2$) is no longer a static constant. Instead, it is redefined as a dynamic variable that scales directly as a function of the athlete's recent training load.Mathematically, the fatigue coefficient at time $t$ is calculated via a secondary impulse-response filter:$$k_2(t) = k_3 \sum_{j=1}^{t-1} w(j) \cdot e^{-(t-j)/\tau_3}$$This seemingly minor adjustment profoundly alters the behavior of forward simulations. As an athlete executes consecutive days of high-intensity training, the dynamic $k_2$ value compounds. Consequently, the performance penalty associated with each additional unit of training load grows exponentially. This represents "training intolerance" or the onset of non-functional overreaching.When optimization algorithms evaluate the Busso model, they no longer prescribe continuous maximal loading. The model's intrinsic mathematical penalty forces the optimizer to naturally generate structured periodization blocks, interspersed with necessary recovery microcycles to allow the $k_2$ coefficient to reset. Furthermore, the model natively outputs exponential and step-down tapering protocols leading into target events, perfectly mirroring the empirically validated practices of elite coaches without requiring arbitrary programmatic enforcement.

### 5.2. The PerPot (Performance Potential) MetamodelDeveloped by Perl at the turn of the century, the Performance Potential (PerPot) metamodel discards the additive logic of the classical FFM entirely. Rather than relying on simple decay constants, PerPot operates as a dynamic, antagonistic state-event model featuring adaptive effect delays.PerPot introduces the concept of internal buffer potentials. A training stimulus acts to drain a specific reserve potential, which must subsequently be refilled through metabolic and structural recovery before an upward adaptation in performance can be realized. The rate at which these potentials drain and refill dictates the athlete's capacity to absorb training.In comparative analyses against the standard FFM utilizing cycling ergometer data, the PerPot model achieved superior predictive accuracy and curve-fitting in a majority of empirical cases. Its primary advantage for forward simulation is its native ability to model both performance stagnation and actual performance degradation (overtraining) resulting from insufficient inter-session recovery, making it a highly robust engine for automated training prescription.

### 5.3. Bayesian Parameter Estimation and the Cold Start ProblemA significant logistical hurdle in deploying forward training simulation is the "cold start" problem. GoldenCheetah's Levenberg-Marquardt optimizer requires a dense history of valid power_index tests to generate $k_1$ and $k_2$ values. For newly onboarded athletes lacking months of historical data, frequentist curve-fitting algorithms will either fail to converge or will return wildly inaccurate parameters.A Bayesian implementation of the impulse-response model solves this initialization dilemma by formalizing the integration of biological priors. Overcoming the Limitations of the Scalar Training ImpulseA profound systemic limitation of the classical Banister implementation—and its contemporary derivations like the Performance Management Chart (PMC)—is the reduction of an entire day's training stress into a single, unidimensional scalar metric, such as Training Stress Score (TSS), TRIMP, or BikeScore.The Violation of SpecificityBy collapsing duration and intensity into a single integer, scalar impulse models fundamentally violate the physiological principle of training specificity. Under a standard scalar FFM, a 3-hour low-intensity Zone 2 endurance ride that accumulates 120 TSS is mathematically indistinguishable from a 60-minute, extremely severe Zone 5 interval session that also accumulates 120 TSS.In biological reality, these two sessions provoke entirely divergent transcriptomic and metabolic cascades. The former predominantly stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis, increased capillarization, and fat oxidation pathways within Type I muscle fibers. The latter intensely challenges intra-cellular buffering capacity, upregulates glycolytic enzyme kinetics, and recruits high-threshold Type IIb motor units. A forward simulator that treats these stimuli identically will fail to accurately predict the subtle performance nuances required for specific event typologies, such as a steady-state 40km time trial versus a highly stochastic criterium race.

## 7. The Hybrid Paradigm: Integrating Mathematical Models with Large Language Models (LLMs)While multi-dimensional impulse-response models paired with Particle Swarm Optimization represent the apex of macro-level training load distribution, mathematical models are inherently blind to the qualitative nuances of human life and the microscopic structuring of athletic performance.A purely mathematical optimizer functions optimally in the domain of energy distribution. It can flawlessly dictate that on Day 42 of a macrocycle, the athlete requires an Aerobic Impulse of 85 units and a Glycolytic Impulse of 15 units to maintain an optimal trajectory toward peak performance. However, mathematics cannot intrinsically format those 100 total units into a biomechanically sound, psychologically engaging workout session. It lacks the semantic reasoning to decide whether the prescribed strain is best achieved through 4 intervals of 8 minutes at 105% CP, or 6 intervals of 5 minutes at 110% CP.Furthermore, mathematical models cannot dynamically process qualitative athlete feedback, such as poor sleep architecture, occupational stress, or feelings of psychological burnout. Attempting to encode these highly variable human elements into a brittle set of mathematical parameters routinely leads to algorithmic over-constraint and failure.

### The Neuro-Symbolic Translation LayerTo bridge the gap between mathematical precision and human-centric coaching, an advanced automated workout generator must adopt a hybrid neuro-symbolic architecture. In this paradigm, the Banister (or 3D-IR) optimization algorithm serves as the deterministic macro-planner, strictly enforcing physiological boundaries and calculating idealized daily strain targets. A Large Language Model (LLM) is then deployed as the tactical micro-executor.Operating via GoldenCheetah's existing /athlete/ai/draft REST API endpoint, the LLM receives the mathematical outputs not as data to be calculated, but as rigid system prompts. The payload acts as a digital straightjacket, providing the LLM with the exact physiological requirements for the day (e.g., Target System: Threshold, Required TRIMP: 110, Max Duration: 90 minutes).Simultaneously, the LLM ingests the athlete's qualitative context—parsing subjective inputs regarding fatigue or time constraints. Utilizing advanced prompting methodologies such as Procedural Guideline Execution Training (PGET), the LLM executes complex sports science logic to synthesize a structured workout. The LLM possesses the semantic awareness to structure appropriate warm-up ramps, calculate work-to-rest ratios that optimize metabolic clearance, and format the final output into GoldenCheetah's native WorkoutDraft JSON schema, replete with specific power targets and progression patterns.In this hybrid ecosystem, the LLM is relieved of the burden of long-term physiological planning—a task it is ill-suited for due to its lack of mathematical forecasting capabilities—while the dynamical systems model is relieved of the burden of human interpretation. The result is a highly adaptive, deeply personalized coaching engine.

## 8. Practical Software Architecture and Minimum Viable Simulation for GoldenCheetahImplementing a fully functional, multi-objective Particle Swarm Optimizer managing a 3D-IR model is a massive software engineering undertaking. To seamlessly transition GoldenCheetah's existing C++ and Qt architecture toward prospective optimization, a phased, incremental approach is necessary.

### Phase 1: The Heuristic Plan Comparator (Minimum Viable Simulation)The highest-leverage initial deployment involves utilizing the Banister model as a comparative evaluation engine for pre-constructed heuristic plans, rather than a de novo plan generator.GoldenCheetah currently tracks daily historical loads via the QVector<double> stress_, lts_, sts_ arrays within the Performance Management Chart (PMC) and calculates future projections via planned_lts_ variables [User Prompt]. Additionally, the QMap<QString, Banister*> banisterData structure already holds the athlete's individually fitted $k_1$, $k_2$, $\tau_1$, and $\tau_2$ parameters.The Minimum Viable Simulation (MVS) executes as follows:Scenario Generation: The workout generator module constructs three to five distinct standardized training sequences bridging the current date and the target event (e.g., a "Polarized" plan, a "Pyramidal" plan, and a "High-Volume Base" plan).Forward Projection: For each candidate plan, the system populates a prospective scalar impulse array (projected daily TSS).Trajectory Simulation: The MVS module iterates the classical Banister equation forward through the arrays, utilizing the athlete's unique parameters pulled from banisterData, projecting the daily evolution of $PTE$, $NTE$, and the resultant predicted performance $p(t)$.Algorithmic Selection: The system evaluates the terminal $p(t)$ value on the target event day. It ranks the heuristic plans based on which sequence mathematically induces the highest performance peak, and selects the optimal structural template for the athlete.This MVS implementation entirely bypasses the need for complex PSO constraint programming, as the heuristic plans are pre-validated for basic physiological safety. It instantly retires the blind ctlFactor scaling currently in use, providing an immediate, individualized upgrade path based on the athlete's mathematical responsiveness to specific load distributions.

### Phase 2: Rolling Re-Optimization and the Sense-Predict-Act LoopA critical biological reality is that an athlete's physiological transfer functions are non-stationary; parameters fitted in January will inevitably drift by April as the athlete's metabolic efficiency and fatigue resistance evolve. A static forward simulation extending months into the future will suffer from progressive predictive decay.To ensure long-term accuracy, GoldenCheetah must implement a closed-loop "sense-predict-act" framework featuring rolling re-optimization.Periodic Refitting: Every 14 to 21 days, the system must trigger a background refitting operation, feeding the latest influx of maximal testing data (via the power_index metrics) back through the Levenberg-Marquardt lmcurve() algorithm.Drift Detection: If the newly fitted parameters diverge significantly from the previous state—for instance, if the $k_1$ fitness multiplier drops, indicating a period of diminished adaptation efficiency—the system flags a state change.Dynamic Recalculation: The forward simulator is triggered to re-evaluate the remainder of the training plan under the new mathematical constraints. If the previously selected trajectory is no longer optimal, the /ai/plan endpoint initiates the LLM translation layer to seamlessly alter the upcoming microcycle, perhaps extending a recovery block to compensate for the newly detected systemic fatigue.

### Phase 3: Validation MethodologyValidating the efficacy of the simulation-based approach against the legacy heuristic system requires rigorous statistical methodologies. Standard machine learning cross-validation techniques must be applied to historical athlete datasets.The most appropriate methodology is Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation, combined with $k$-fold time-series analysis. The algorithm is trained on a subset of the athlete's historical data (e.g., the first 16 weeks of a season) to establish baseline parameters. The forward simulator then predicts the performance outcomes for the subsequent 8 weeks, given the known training loads executed during that period. The accuracy of the simulation is quantified using Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) metrics.Open-source reference implementations, such as the R-based dorem (Dose-Response Modeling) package developed by sports scientists, provide robust architectures for conducting these exact cross-validation routines on Banister models and can be translated to benchmark the C++ implementation within GoldenCheetah.

## 9. Key Risks and Essential MitigationsEntrusting the distribution of profound physiological strain to an automated mathematical optimization engine carries non-trivial risks. If improperly constrained, algorithms relentlessly prioritize mathematical maximums over biological preservation.

### The Overtraining Escalation LoopBecause the linear Banister model computes the decay of fatigue ($\tau_2 \approx 11$ days) much faster than the decay of fitness ($\tau_1 \approx 50$ days), an unconstrained optimizer will inevitably deduce that massive, unprecedented spikes in training load immediately prior to a taper period are mathematically "optimal". It calculates that the catastrophic fatigue generated by a 1500 TSS week will vanish just before race day, leaving behind a massive spike in fitness. Biologically, such an extreme overload would result in immune system suppression, severe muscle damage, and acute overtraining syndrome, effectively destroying the athlete's performance.

**Mitigation Strategy:** The generation loop must incorporate hard-coded, immutable circuit breakers that override the Banister performance predictions. The WorkoutAthleteSnapshot must continuously evaluate the trajectory of the Acute Training Load (ATL) against the Chronic Training Load (CTL). If the forward simulation proposes a daily workload that drives the projected Training Stress Balance (TSB) below an absolute floor of $-30$, or demands a CTL ramp rate exceeding 6 points per week, the algorithm must instantly abort and penalize that simulation pathway, regardless of its theoretical yield.

### The "Garbage In, Garbage Out" ParadigmThe entire foundation of prospective training optimization is predicated on the fidelity of the $k_1$ and $k_2$ parameters. If an athlete's historical dataset is contaminated by poorly calibrated power meters, missing data files, or a refusal to engage in regular maximal testing efforts, the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm will converge on statistical artifacts. An optimizer simulating forward on noise will prescribe a chaotic and potentially harmful training sequence.

**Mitigation Strategy:** GoldenCheetah must implement strict data-quality gating logic. The system must actively scan the athlete's power_index history prior to initiating any forward simulation. If the software detects an absence of diverse, maximal physiological efforts across the preceding 6-week window—or detects severe data anomalies—it must refuse to engage the individualized Banister optimization. In such instances, the software must gracefully degrade, falling back either to the legacy CTL-heuristic or to generalized Bayesian population priors until a reliable, high-fidelity parameter fit can be established through future testing.
